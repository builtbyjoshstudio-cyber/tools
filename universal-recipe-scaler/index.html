Chef's Note: Volumetric baking measurements (cups) become highly inaccurate when scaled up massively due to compaction. For bulletproof professional results, consider converting your core ingredients to weight (grams).

A few things I've learned scaling recipes:

Scaling the numbers is the easy part — this tool handles that. What the math won't tell you is that some things don't scale 1:1. Salt and strong spices are the big ones: double a recipe and you usually want a little less than double the salt, then adjust at the end by tasting. Same with garlic and chili — they get louder in bulk.

Liquids and reductions are the other trap. If you scale up a sauce, you've got more liquid to cook off, so go by how it looks and coats a spoon, not by the original time. A bigger batch almost always takes longer to reduce than you'd expect.

And when in doubt, scale by weight instead of volume. Cups get unreliable fast when you're multiplying — a scale is the cheapest upgrade to your cooking I can think of.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you scale a recipe up or down?

Multiply every ingredient quantity by the same factor — doubling a recipe means multiplying each amount by two. The tricky part is keeping the ratios consistent and rounding to amounts you can actually measure, which this tool handles automatically.

Can I convert a recipe between metric and imperial?

Yes. Paste a recipe in either system and switch the output toggle — the tool converts weights, volumes, temperatures, and lengths between metric and US customary units as it scales.

Should I scale spices and salt at the same rate as everything else?

Not always. Strong seasonings, salt, garlic, and leavening often don't scale linearly — doubling a recipe rarely needs exactly double the cayenne. Scale them, then taste and adjust toward the lower end.

Does scaling change cooking times?

Usually yes for volume-dependent steps but not always proportionally. A doubled cake batter in a bigger pan bakes longer; a doubled sauce may simmer about the same. Use times as a starting point and judge by doneness.
