How dough hydration is calculated

Dough hydration is the weight of water in a dough expressed as a percentage of the flour — not of the total dough. This is the heart of baker's percentages: flour is always the 100% baseline, and every other ingredient is measured against it. A dough with 1,000g of flour and 720g of water is at 72% hydration. Working this way means a recipe scales cleanly to any batch size and stays consistent regardless of the units you use, which is why bakers measure by weight rather than by cups.

This calculator works in that direction: set your target hydration and the batch size you want — either a total dough weight or a flour weight — and it returns the exact flour, water, salt, and yeast by weight. Salt and yeast are set as their own baker's percentages of the flour, the same way a written formula would express them. For alkaline noodle doughs it swaps yeast for kansui, and for egg pasta it offers an egg-for-water conversion, since those doughs are built differently from leavened bread.

Hydration isn't a free dial, though — every flour has a practical absorption range. A lean all-purpose flour turns slack and sticky pushed much past 65%, Tipo 00 has a lower ceiling than high-gluten bread flour, and noodle doughs stay deliberately stiff, often under 48%, so they can be rolled and cut. The calculator flags when your target falls outside the workable range for the flour you've chosen, so you can adjust before you mix rather than discovering it with your hands in the bowl.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is dough hydration?

Hydration is the weight of water in a dough expressed as a percentage of the flour weight. A 70% hydration dough has 700g of water for every 1000g of flour. Higher hydration generally means a more open, airy crumb.

What is baker's percentage?

Baker's percentage expresses every ingredient as a percentage of the total flour weight, which is always 100%. It lets you scale a formula to any batch size while keeping the ratios identical.

How much hydration does my bread need?

It depends on the style — pizza and bagels run lower, ciabatta and rustic sourdough run much higher. This tool includes presets for common styles, such as Neapolitan at 60%, baguette at 65%, sourdough at 72%, and ciabatta at 82%, as starting points.

Why does my high-hydration dough feel too sticky?

Different flours absorb different amounts of water. If a dough is unmanageable, the flour may be near its absorption ceiling — the tool flags when your target exceeds what a given flour typically handles.
